C. How was the community affected by the typhoon?

The typhoons caused extensive damage due to their powerful winds, torrential rainfall, and storm surges, which resulted in severe flooding and landslides. Many cities and communities experienced widespread destruction, with some areas losing as much as 90% of housing. Thousands of people lost their lives and 4.1 million people were left displaced from their homes. Yolanda not only destroyed their houses but also ruined their livelihoods, which were primarily based on coconut farming, the Philippines' biggest source of income. The typhoon destroyed coconut farms all over the islands, leaving over a million coconut farmers without a means to support themselves as they had to wait years for their farms to recover. The typhoon also affected the local fisheries, which is one of the Philippines' most important sectors, employing many families who lost everything during Typhoon Yolanda and were left struggling once the storm was over.

E. How vulnerable are they to typhoons?

The destructive consequences of typhoons can be overwhelming, particularly for those residing in the impacted regions. These consequences can vary from physical damage to loss of human lives. The regions that encounter typhoons are confronted by numerous challenges and difficulties, such as damage to infrastructure, power outages, flooding, landslides, and disruption of transportation systems. The aftermath of these calamities can have far-reaching impacts that necessitate substantial resources and assistance for recovery. Vegetation cover and landform explain vulnerability better than the average slope in most instances. For different loss types the dominant socioeconomic vulnerability factor is different. For both typhoons, emergency transfer has a higher determining power ranking for the population vulnerability, while the percentage of the GDP made up of primary industry has higher ranking for the economic vulnerability. The dominant interaction effects between two vulnerability factors differ depending on the typhoon and loss type but show a nonlinear enhancement effect in most cases.

H. Were there any positive impacts of the typhoon in the region? What are those?

While typhoons are typically associated with problems and harm to communities, there can be some beneficial outcomes in the aftermath. One such benefit is that these storms can aid in the replenishment of freshwater supplies by delivering much-needed rainfall to drought-stricken regions, can deposit nutrient-rich sediment onto agricultural lands, increase soil fertility and crop yields for future purposes, and can also play a crucial role in shaping and reshaping coastlines, which may ultimately benefit various ecosystems. And most importantly, experiencing the effects of typhoons can raise awareness and prepare us for future natural disasters. This can spur communities to implement stronger emergency response plans and improve overall disaster management infrastructure. Despite that, It is crucial to take notes about the negative effects that typhoons can have on living beings and their natural surroundings. It is important not to normalize or underestimate the damage caused by these natural disasters.
